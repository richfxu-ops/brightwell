The problem you actually have

You said three things, and they're really one thing:

  • "I have trouble coming up with card designs from scratch."
  • "I don't understand all the mechanics very well and need reminders."
  • "I'm thinking of a card design screen in the codex, but I'm not sure what it would look like."

Here's the reframe that professional card designers would give you: nobody good designs cards from scratch. "From scratch" — a blank page, infinite options, all constraints held in your head — is the hardest possible way to design anything, and the pros have spent thirty years building tools specifically so they never have to do it. The trouble you're having isn't a talent gap; it's a tooling gap. So let's look at the tools.

How Magic: The Gathering actually designs cards

Magic is the most-studied case in card design because its head designer, Mark Rosewater, has written a weekly column about the craft for over twenty years. A few of their core practices are directly stealable for Brightwell.

1 · Design happens in a pipeline, not a moment

A Magic set passes through four named stages: Exploratory Design (is this theme even fun?), Vision Design (what is this set about — its themes and signature mechanics), Set Design (actually making the several hundred cards), and Play Design (a dedicated balance team that plays the set competitively for months and sets the final numbers). The insight: inventing what a card does and deciding its numbers are different jobs, done by different processes, at different times. A designer sketching a card is explicitly not responsible for getting the cost right on the first try.

2 · The design skeleton — you fill slots, you don't face blank pages

This is the single most useful idea for you. Before any individual card is designed, the lead builds a design skeleton: a spreadsheet of empty slots, one per card the set needs, each slot pre-labeled with a role — "cheap white creature," "common removal spell," "card that supports the graveyard theme," "big splashy rare." Designing a card then means picking a slot and answering a much smaller question: "what's a fun way to be the common removal spell in a ghost set?" That question has maybe a dozen good answers. "Design a card" has infinite answers, which is why it paralyzes.

You already have a skeleton and may not have noticed: the Eveners design brief defines role slots — filler, builder, payoff, supports — with numeric bands per tier. The way-rebuild plan calls for one brief per Way. A skeleton is those briefs turned into a checklist of empty slots you can point at.

3 · The color pie — identity through restriction

Magic's five colors each have a philosophy, a list of things they're best at, and — crucially — a list of things they are never allowed to do. Red doesn't get card-draw-and-heal; white doesn't get "destroy anything cheaply." Rosewater calls the color pie the most important part of Magic's design, and his most-repeated lesson is "restrictions breed creativity." The restrictions aren't a cage around design; they're what makes each color feel like something — and what hands a stuck designer their starting point.

Your color pie already exists and it's stricter than Magic's: six Walking Ways with locked identities, plus hard firewalls in the vocabulary (brim is the only gleam-writer; soothe never touches gleam; fills read chain for Eveners and bare grain only for Untold…). What's missing is having those laws in front of you while you design, instead of spread across the GDD, D-025, and the vocabulary file.

4 · A complexity budget — simple by default, depth on purpose

Since 2011 Magic has run an explicit policy ("New World Order"): cards at common rarity must stay simple enough that a newer player can read the battlefield; complexity is a budget spent deliberately at higher rarities. They even have vocabulary for it — a "vanilla" card has no text at all, and a "lenticular" card reads simple to a beginner while hiding depth for an expert. Your vocabulary already encodes this: warm and keep are explicitly no-combo "legible baseline" primitives, and your card-quality rubric's decision rules are a complexity budget in embryo.

5 · Who is this card for?

Magic designs against player archetypes — Timmy wants to experience something big, Johnny wants to build something clever, Spike wants to win efficiently. Every card is aimed at someone. Your single-player equivalent is the brief's "decision texture" bullets: a card earns its slot by creating a fork the player feels ("room now vs money later"). A card that creates no decision is aimed at no one.

6 · Cards live in a database and iterate constantly

Internally, every in-progress Magic card lives in a shared database (they've called it Multiverse) where any R&D member can comment on any card; cards carry joke placeholder names for months; numbers change dozens of times; and the balance team (playing in the "Future Future League") feeds results back in. No card is right the first time, by design of the process. Your equivalents: the card JSON pool, the codex Data Viewer, the sim harness + exploit bot, and the card-audit dashboard. Your iteration loop already exists — it's the sketching front-end that doesn't.

The key decision: how much of the pipeline lives in the browser?

This is the one-way-door-ish choice, so it's yours. Three shapes:

Option A — Sketchpad. The Workbench produces sketches, not finished cards. You fill the form, copy the sketch block, hand it to me in chat; I compile it to JSON over the primitives, run validate.ts + the rubric lint, and sim the batch — exactly the division of labor D-024 already locked ("the user is the card designer; the agent organizes"). The form can still give soft, contextual warnings (pick brim as an Evener and it turns red), but the real firewall stays in one place: the TypeScript.

Why this beats B: no duplicated rule logic to drift out of sync. Cost of being wrong: low — if sketch-only proves too weak, A upgrades into B later; the baked-data plumbing is the same.
Option A+ — Sketchpad with a mechanical compile (recommended). Everything in A, plus a "Compile to JSON" button. This is not Option B in disguise, because the button only does serialization: the form's fields already mirror the card schema (name, grain, mark, ceiling, tags, effects[{when, do, params}], the plain-English note), so emitting the JSON is formatting, not judgment. No rule logic moves into the browser. The emitted card is explicitly a draft — it still goes through the real pipeline (validate.ts + card-lint + batch sim) before it can enter the pool; the browser never gets to say "legal."

Two requirements keep this honest:
  • Rules as shared data. The Way-filtered dropdowns need to know per-Way laws ("Eveners never brim"). Those laws should live in one small data file (alongside design/vocabulary.json), baked into the codex and consumed by card-lint — one source of truth, two readers, no drift. This new file is the real decision to sign off.
  • Escape hatches per field. Some params are genuinely fiddly (amounts with read(...) per/by syntax, targets like inert:hand). Any param can drop back to plain English; the compile emits it as a TODO the agent resolves. The form never blocks you on the hard 10%.
Option B — Full builder. The form produces final card JSON with live validation replicated in JavaScript inside the codex. More self-serve power, but it means maintaining a second copy of validate.ts + card-lint inside a 3,000-line single-file HTML — every future rule change lands twice or the tool starts lying to you. A tool that lies about legality is worse than no tool. Cost of being wrong: permanent double maintenance.
Option C — No screen. Keep working from the brief docs + chat. Cheapest, and the briefs are genuinely good — but it leaves all seven documents open in tabs and the blank page undefeated. Given you've now hit this wall twice (the pause note in way-rebuild's plan is literally "next step is the user's first card sketches"), I don't think C holds.

Suggested build order (if you pick A)

Three small, separately shippable chunks — each useful on its own, matching your small-reviewable-pieces preference:

  • Chunk 1 — the reading half: Workbench tab with Identity panel + Crib sheet + Slot board, all baked from existing sources (briefs, vocabulary.json, D-025 tier table). No new interactivity. This alone kills the "reminders" problem.
  • Chunk 2 — the sketch form: the guided form with Way-filtered dropdowns and the copy-out sketch block. This kills the blank page. If A+: the "Compile to JSON" button lands here too — it makes this the biggest chunk, since each primitive needs a small param widget (with the plain-English escape hatch for the fiddly ones).
  • Chunk 3 — the precedent shelf: role-filtered card list with the audit dashboard's telemetry columns. This teaches taste.
One honest dependency: the slot board needs each Way's brief to exist, and only the Eveners brief is written. That's fine — the Workbench can launch Eveners-only, and each new brief lights up its Way. It also means the Workbench creates pull to finish the briefs, which is the order way-rebuild wanted anyway.
