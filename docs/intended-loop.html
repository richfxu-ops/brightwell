How Brightwell is meant to play

…and what "everything's already awoken" is supposed to feel like — vs. what the toy does today.

Your question — "if you pour just to wake cost, what happens once the cards are all awoken?" — is the hinge the whole design turns on. The short version: waking a card isn't finishing with it. A woken card becomes permanent audience, and audience is the engine.

The intended turn, one morning

  1. Dawn. You get income: a base, plus what your woken audience seats into the room, plus yesterday's unspent attention that waited on this table. Draw a hand.
  2. The room gathers. Your attention pool for the morning = the draw + every woken piece you own seating the room. More woken pieces → a bigger room, every single dawn. This is the compounding.
  3. Play a chain. Spend attention to play pieces; playing back-to-back keeps the room high (a stall lets it cool). Budget and momentum pulling against each other.
  4. Wake / operate. Pour onto a cold piece past its mark and it wakes — fired forever, and it joins the audience. Replay an already-woken piece and its on-play effects fire again (gather, fill, brim, draw…).
  5. Overflow → Standing. Push a play past a card's ceiling and the excess runs to your Standing (gleam). "A full cup runs to the pourer." The biggest gleam swings happen here.
  6. Fulfill. Fill an asking's need and the town re-makes — you're paid the glad-load (money, a taught new piece, materials).

So — "everything's awoken." Now what?

In the intended design, that's not an ending, it's the moment your machine is finally on. A fully-woken deck is a big automatic room every dawn, and you spend that room by replaying your woken pieces for their effects. You are no longer building the engine — you're running it:

The room compounds — more woken audience means a larger pool each morning, so your plays land harder over time.

You keep reaching for overflow — pouring a woken piece past its ceiling turns surplus room into Standing. That's a repeatable payoff you chase every morning, not a one-time thing.

Fills scale with the engine — after our recent change, fills read woken:<suit>, your accumulated audience of a grain. So a big woken deck pours a big fill (this is why the count no longer "dries up").

New pieces keep arriving — the Fair offers fresh cards each dawn, so there's always something new to draft and wake on top of the engine you have.

And the reason you can't just build one big room and coast: the askings grow with the year. The season "seep" raises the minimum need every leg (1 → 3 → 5 → 7), and answering brightly ratchets future needs higher still. A static engine gets out-grown by the weather — so you must keep reaching, which is exactly where Standing goes at risk. That tension (grow-and-risk vs. play-safe-and-be-outgrown) is the intended heartbeat, and the year-end crown (now demand-20) is the finale the whole rising curve climbs toward: you can only stand it if you grew.

The honest gap: the toy is a single node

Here's the part worth being straight about — what you're feeling in the toy is real, and it's a known deferral, not a mystery. The intended pressure that keeps a woken engine honest through the mid-game is mostly the systems we haven't built yet:

routing / skip-arithmetic — deferred the combing boss — deferred multiple towns / the map — deferred

The toy plays one node. So you never route between towns, never choose which pale needs to let ripen into bigger pleas, and never face the combing (the world-scale great asking that a skipped region grows into). Those are the mid-game teeth.

What the toy does have: ordinary askings that grow by season (1→7) and with your peak. But a mature woken engine blows past a need-of-7 easily — so once your engine exceeds the weather floor, the ordinary askings go trivial and nothing is genuinely hard until the Wintering crown. That trough is the flatness you're noticing. In the full game the routing + combing fill that trough; in the toy it's empty.

So, plainly

Intended: "all awoken" = your engine is online; you operate it (replay for effects, chase overflow→Standing, pour fills that scale with your woken audience), keep drafting new pieces from the Fair, and keep reaching because the weather and the crown demand a bigger engine than you have.

Today, in the toy: the operate-the-engine half is real and now works (woken audience seats the room, on-play fills scale, overflow pays Standing). The keep-being-pressured half is thin, because the routing/combing/map that supply mid-game pressure are deferred — so a woken deck coasts from "engine online" to "the crown" with little in between.

If we wanted the toy to feel the intended loop sooner without building the whole map, the cheapest lever would be a second/third town to route between (so skipping and the spiral-clock come alive) — a much smaller build than the full meta-layer. Happy to scope that if it's worth it.
