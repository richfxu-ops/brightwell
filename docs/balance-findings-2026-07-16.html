Balance Findings

2026-07-16 · from the per-card telemetry + exploit cohort (D-023) · 700 bot runs (7 archetypes × 2 strategies × 50 seeds) · evidence browsable in the card-audit dashboard
TL;DR. Your playtest instinct is confirmed and quantified. A deliberately dumb bot that ignores every archetype's identity and just spams fill cards wins as often or more often than the identity bots for all seven archetypes. One card — The Long Tally — single-handedly produces 15% of everything the exploit strategy fills. Meanwhile 15 of 97 cards are dead (in the deck all run, almost never worth playing), and two Ways (Eveners, Mannerly) barely function even when played as designed. The good news: zero cards break the rubric's structural rules anymore — this is now a numbers problem and a variety problem, not a rules problem, and we have the instruments to verify every fix.

How this evidence was gathered

Every simulated run now records what each card actually did (drafted, played, woken, and — new — exactly how much asking-fill each card produced). On top of the six identity bots, an exploit bot plays the degenerate strategy a min-maxing human would: fill first, always, draft more fill, ignore the Way's identity. Comparing the two cohorts turns "the game feels too easy" into a number.

One honest caveat: the per-card win delta ("do runs containing this card win more?") is a correlation, not proof of cause — a card drafted by already-winning runs looks good. That's why the findings below triangulate: exploit-cohort fill share, play rates, dead flags, and the rubric all have to point the same way before a card gets named.

Finding 2 — The cards that trivialize the need

  • The Long Tally (Untold capstone) — fills by your woken-thread count, which Untold grows all run. It alone contributes 15% of all fill in the entire exploit cohort (next card: under 10%), and runs containing it win +46% more. The scaling rule it uses is the sanctioned one (R3-compliant); the coefficient is simply too generous. Prime nerf candidate.
  • Ring Up the Lanterns (Fairwrights support, mark 2) — seats as many newcomers as the room already holds, i.e. a room snowball for almost nothing. Off-Way drafters love it: Kilnfast runs that picked it up won +54% more. A cheap universal room-doubler undermines every Way's identity at once — either bound it or price it like the engine piece it is.
  • Feed and Fire / Comb the Verge — same shape, milder: strong universal builders whose value travels to any deck (+39–45% deltas in other Ways' cohorts). Worth watching after the two above are tuned.

Finding 3 — Fifteen dead cards

These are in a deck all run and essentially never get played (under 3% of plays, rule R14). They cluster into three repair groups:

  1. Reading systems that don't exist yet — walk-the-pale, pale-harvest, mend-the-verge (Gleaners) read spiral/season, which the engine lists as deferred systems. They're reading zeros. Give them an interim value or re-point the read until those systems land.
  2. Known offenders awaiting their D-022 fix — even-the-rim, plus supports like seasoned-timber, silver-warm-up: the telemetry independently re-derived your playtest fix-list, which is good news for trusting the instrument.
  3. Shared filler-chaff — first-step, grey-bowl, hearth-hum, kettle-loaf, loose-thread, pegged-bench, sprig-trestle: generic low-impact cards that lose every comparison with a Way card. These need a real job or a cut.

Finding 4 — Two Ways don't work even played as designed

Eveners (8% wins) and Mannerly (16%) are the flat archetypes. The telltale detail: inside Mannerly's own losing cohort, its two song-count fillers (the-refrain-repeated +49%, hold-the-note +40%) are the strongest cards in the entire pool by win-delta — meaning the kit has exactly one working win-path and everything else in it is drag. Eveners' chain-fill path (even-the-rim) is so unattractive the bots barely play it. These two kits need their identity loops completed, not just numbers nudged (rubric R7: a repeatable filler + a builder + a payoff, all seeded and all worth playing).

What to do about it — proposed fix order (Step 4)

  1. Nerf the two trivializers — The Long Tally's coefficient, Ring Up the Lanterns' snowball — and re-run the sim. Success = exploit gaps shrink, especially the +32 apprentice line.
  2. Repair the 15 dead cards by group — re-point the zero-reads, apply the D-022 fixes, give the shared chaff real jobs. Success = dead count trends toward 0.
  3. Complete the Eveners and Mannerly identity loops so their designed play beats fill-spam. Success = each Way's identity line ≥ its exploit line.
  4. Only then touch global difficulty knobs (crown demand, asking scaling) — tuning difficulty while cards are broken just bakes the brokenness in.

Each step is verified the same way: npm run sim && npm run audit, then check the gap table and the flag counts. The dead/dominant thresholds and the exploit gap are now regression metrics — a card change that makes them worse fails visibly.

Scope note: all numbers are bot floors (deliberately simple policies, D-016) on the simplified single-map engine — they understate what a clever human can do, so the exploit gaps are conservative.
