What this is. The log of design choices already made — newest first, each with a code like D-014 and the reason behind it, in plain terms. The entries at the bottom (D-001–D-008) set the ground rules before the game existed. New here? The Glossary tab decodes the in-world words.

D-022 Card-quality rulebook finalised Jul 14

Locked the four remaining rules for judging cards (see §H on the Questions tab).

Why: so an automated card-checker can enforce a single, agreed standard.

D-021 Late-game cards now read your whole audience Jul 14

Fixed three balance bugs found by playtesting: a card that trivially won the crown in one shot, cards that mysteriously stopped working late in a run, and one class that rewarded the wrong strategy.

Why: the "reward" cards counted only this turn's activity, which dries up once your deck is fully woken. Re-pointing them at your accumulated audience makes them scale with the engine you built all game. The final boss's demand was raised 10→20 to keep it a real climb.

D-020 Card pool doubled (49 → 97) Jul 14

Roughly doubled the number of cards, and made the new ones draft-only (not in your starting deck).

Why: a small deck gets boring once every card is woken. A deeper pool means there's always something new to chase, while keeping starting decks lean so your draft choices — not a fixed deck — drive each run's variety. Result: every one of the six classes can now win (two previously couldn't).

D-019 More cards flow through the Fair Jul 14

Widened the draft (5→7 cards offered, take up to 3 per turn instead of 2).

Why: to ease the "everything's woken, now what?" plateau by keeping fresh cards moving.

D-018 The lose-condition got teeth Jul 14

Tightened how fast wasted effort converts into safety score, so reputation stops ballooning to untouchable levels.

Why: tests showed the safety resource maxed out trivially and the run was never actually threatened — a game with no real danger. This cut typical peak score nearly in half.

D-017 Filler cards fire every time you play them Jul 14

Changed key cards to trigger on play rather than once on waking.

Why: firing only once made mature decks go passive and encouraged a boring "hoard the card until the finale" tactic. This single change took the win rate from 0 to ~11 in 350 — proving earlier zero-win results were this trap, not broken numbers.

D-016 Built the balance-testing robot Jul 14

Created the simulation that plays thousands of bot games and records detailed stats.

Why: so balance decisions rest on real measured numbers instead of back-of-napkin guesses. Deliberately uses simple bots, so the numbers are treated as a floor, not gospel.

D-015 The deck can now grow Jul 14

Built acquisition (the Fair, earned cards, and a way to thin the deck), letting a starting ~7 cards grow to ~20.

Why: a run needs to build an engine big enough to stand the crown. Also resolved how you pay for cards: cheap ones bought with money, the best ones won by performing.

D-014 The run now ends — three ways Jul 14

Added win/lose conditions: lose your reputation any season → the Quiet Walk; reach the final still morning → win if you stood the crown, otherwise you "drift."

Why: it was previously an endless sandbox. A static deck literally cannot win, which closes the "turtle and never risk anything" exploit.

D-013 Card-drafting adopted — "the Fair" Jul 14

Decided that acquiring cards should be a frequent, woven-in draft rather than a rare seasonal event.

Why: playtesting showed a small fully-woken deck plateaus — nothing new to do. Frequent drafting keeps every turn interesting.

D-012 Contract lifecycle built Jul 14

Implemented accepting, fulfilling, escalating, and failing quests, and set the penalties for flopping one.

Why: to make contracts a real pressure system with genuine downside, not a stub.

D-011 The game is titled Brightwell Jul 14

"Roundelay" stays the name of the world; the game itself becomes Brightwell.

Why: it rolls off the tongue better, and it has an in-world meaning — a town's pool of kept light, which is exactly what a maker leaves behind.

D-010 The five rules of a turn Jul 14

Settled how a single morning plays, decided one rule at a time (see §B on the Questions tab).

Why: these five rules together define the moment-to-moment feel of playing a turn.

D-009 A card-counting rule clarified Jul 13

Defined precisely what two "count my cards" effects measure — one counts this turn's activity, the other counts your accumulated audience across turns.

Why: a clarification, not a change — it matches how the locked design's example cards already work.

D-008 Engine ground rules Jul 13

The technical spine: the game state is one plain object changed by pure functions; every change is logged; randomness comes from a single seeded source; early versions may simplify detail but never bend the rules.

Why: a deterministic, logged engine is testable and reproducible — the same game replays exactly.

D-007 Tooling choices Jul 13

TypeScript in strict mode, modern Node, a lightweight test runner, no heavy build tooling yet.

Why: keep the toolchain minimal until there's actual engine code to justify more.

D-001 – D-006 The founding rules Jul 9–10

The pre-game decisions from the design pipeline: it's a single-player roguelike deckbuilder built around escalating contracts and a depletable reputation (D-001); the aesthetic is wonder / whimsy / elegance, never grimdark (D-002); a list of worlds it must not resemble (D-003); plus setup and naming ratifications (D-004–D-006).

Why: these are the locked foundation everything else is built on.
