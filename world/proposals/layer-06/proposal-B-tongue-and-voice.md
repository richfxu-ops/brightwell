# Layer 6 Proposal B — THE PLAIN BRIGHT TONGUE

**Lens: tongue and voice.** The language comes first, because in Roundelay it always did: the world's strangest laws already live in the homeliest words — *askings, handsels, gleanings, waymeets, even-morrows* — and every visual motif this layer paints is a word made visible. This proposal codifies how Roundelay grows a name, rules what may never be named, gives the cards a specific speaker with a specific warmth, executes the sanctioned renames (I-002, I-047, and the turn-of-a-Round collision), settles I-016 as a style law, and delivers the visual bible system by system — palette words, light behaviors, shape language, motion signatures an artist can execute and check any frame against.

**The layer's one-sentence creed:** *Roundelay names its greatest things the way a kitchen names its spoons, and paints its smallest things the way a church paints its windows — and the whole aesthetic is the distance between those two gestures.*

---

## PART I — THE NAMING LANGUAGE

### How a name is grown

Nothing in Roundelay is christened. There is no officiant to do it, no roll to enter it on, and no ceremony the register would permit — so names arrive the way road-marks arrive: someone says the thing plainly, the road repeats it, hearth by hearth, and one year it turns out to have been the name all along, *checked until it was so*. A name in Roundelay is therefore never an announcement. It is a worn tool — and like every worn tool it shows its work. This is the layer's deepest register rule, and every rule below is a case of it: **a name is grown from the work, never assigned from above.**

The tongue has five roots. Every coin this layer mints — and every coin any later document mints — must grow from one of them.

**1. The worked -ing.** Craft names its verbs, then keeps them as nouns: *the asking, the telling, the courting, the combing, the gleaning, the wintering, the vouching, the showing, the sizing, the setting-out.* The world names *processes*, never institutions — there is no Guild of Gleaners and no Office of the Lantern, there is only *the gleaning* and *the keeping of the lantern*, work anyone might be doing this season. When a new system needs a name, noun its verb first and see if you are already done.

**2. The yoked pair.** New things are named by hitching two old words: *asking-lantern, journey-piece, year-string, gloaming-table, road-mark, even-morrow, waymeet, hearth-right, luck-bird, net-purse, shavings-jar, glad-load.* The yoke is always plain English to plain English — never Latin, never Greek, never an invented phoneme anywhere in the world. A child hearing a yoked pair for the first time should be able to guess what it is and be right.

**3. The temper-name.** A material's name states its terms: *singing-silver* must be sung to, *laughing-riverwood* opens for laughter, *festival-glass* keeps its crowd, *last-red* is what the hearth-line leaves last. The name is the notebook entry compressed to two words — a rule with a courtship inside it — and it is why a maker can walk into a strange town and already half-know the stone.

**4. The gift-word.** When the tongue needs a coin it does not invent; it *revives* — small homely archaisms kept warm by use: *handsel* (a first gift for luck, a real old word doing real work), *glean, morrow, widdershins, waymeet, handsel's* cousin *hansel-night* if we ever need one. The rule for writers: before coining, go shopping in the attic of English. The word that feels inevitable usually already exists, slightly dusty, and reviving it makes the world feel older than any invention could.

**5. The homely great** *(the scale-inversion law)*. The greater the thing, the smaller its name. The reserve of all spent light under the world is *the gloaming-table* — named like kitchen furniture. The countryside's own voice arriving at world-scale is *a great asking* — the same word as a tin spoon on a lantern. The nearest thing the world has to a miracle is *a closed round* — a phrase you could use about a song. This is the register's strongest defense against both grandeur and twee: Roundelay is never allowed a Doom, an Aurora, or an Eldertide. If a proposed name would look at home on a fantasy map poster, it is wrong; if it would look at home on a seed packet or a joiner's shelf, it is probably right.

### What gets a compound name, and what gets a personal name

- **People get personal names, and only people.** Maker names run homely and hedgerow: plant and small-thing first names (*Sorrel, Immy, Bryony, Tansy, Alder, Rush, Fern*), short worn surnames of ground and weather (*Ash, Vane, Fell, Wold, Frost, Lea*). No epithets, no titles, no "the Great" — the road says *her crown, the year she walked the west wheel*, and that is as close to a title as the world gets.
- **Places get grown toponyms** — English hedgerow morphology (*-by, -mere, -fold, -low, -ford, -fell, -down, -thorpe, X-under-Y, X-on-the-Z*), each with a small true thing inside it (see samples below).
- **Ways get gait-names** — *the Kilnfast, the Untold, the Eveners* — plural, lowercase-hearted, describing how they walk, never what they hold (they hold nothing).
- **Made things get work-names, never person-names.** See the law below — this is I-003's home.

### What is never named — the silences of the tongue

The register bans are not a style sheet bolted onto the language; they are holes where the words would be, and the layer states them as such:

- **Things are never christened** *(this is the I-003 ruling, executed)*. Nothing made ever receives a person-name — no Bess the kettle, no naming your fiddle. A made thing is called by its **work and its place**: *the Hask bell, the Setterby stair, my mother's third kiln, the door at Wader's Ford.* The reason is exact and canonical: **nothing in Roundelay speaks except people**, and a person-name is a small pretend mouth. Give a kettle a name and a player will wait for it to talk; call it *the old kettle in the rafters* and it can hum forever and never once break the restraint ban. Woken things attend — and the tongue attends back by naming their work, which is the only self they have. (Corollary for card text and UI: no made thing is ever quoted, addressed by name, or given a point of view. The fiddle is *her grandmother's fiddle* in the winter-telling and in the collection screen alike.)
- **There are no offices.** *Lantern-keeper* is a household turn taken like a verse of the song, spelled lowercase, never capitalized into a rank. The tongue has no words for warden, marshal, guildmaster, or clerk, and no layer may need them.
- **There are no theonyms.** The world's biggest phenomena wear its smallest names (root 5), and nothing anywhere is worshipped by name. The cheerful heresy is *the cheerful heresy* — even the world's one big question is named like a bit of porch teasing.
- **The banned vocabularies have no diegetic synonyms.** Nothing is owed, due, filed, fined, or paid; the tongue routes around the holes with its own words, and the routing is half the world's charm: not *price* but **the read**; not *payment* but **the glad-load**; not *deadline* but **out of season**; not *reputation* but **what the road has heard**; not *quest log* but **the year-string**; not *deposit* hmm— the tongue simply has no such thought, and card text must never need one.
- **The old name of the tide is retired** (see Part III).

### Sample names

**A starter hand (journey-pieces, apprentice tier — ten cards).** Suits shown in parentheses; every name is a thing you could hold, per canon's cards-as-physical-fragments law.

1. **First Whittle** (whittling) — a withy blank from the dooryard hedge; the money faucet in a child's first gesture.
2. **The Folded Joke** (paper) — canon's paper bird; unfolding it tells it, once. The starter exhaust card.
3. **Three Bars of a Planing-Song** (song/joinery) — canon's knotted cord, kept exactly as coined.
4. **Ribbon of the Turning Dance** (dance) — the turn of a dance held in a ribbon.
5. **Weather-Talk** (courting) — the piece that opens river clay: you tell it about the weather; it has been down there all year.
6. **Kettle-Hum** (hearth) — settle the room's oldest woken kettle into its low attentive hum; the starter attention pip.
7. **The Even Rim** (clay) — the thumb-stroke that evens a bowl's lip; teaches the working edge before the player knows they are learning it.
8. **Chalk-Bird** (road) — the first road-mark of every maker's life, pointing outward; the starter scout.
9. **The Borrowed Laugh** (dough) — bread rises for a room in a good mood; the starter mood-token.
10. **A Crust for the Road** (dough) — the heel of the loaf pressed on a leaving guest; small heal, small warmth, given not kept.

**Towns and roads (eight towns, four roads).** Canon already holds *Ferrow, Merrylees, Whistledown, Brindle-on-the-Wold, Setterby, Hask, Quill Vale, Wray Fell, Wader's Ford*; these extend the same soil:

- **Withyfold** — a bend of pollard willows; whittling country.
- **Emberlow** — a low hill that keeps its hearth-line red late; gleaners descend to it at dusk.
- **Glazeby** — kiln-town on the east wheel; the Kilnfast sing its water.
- **Swanmere** — a ferry mere; festival-glass wintering ground.
- **Dovelow** — a quiet porch famous three sheds around; forecasting country.
- **Rushford Slip** — a laughing-riverwood landing; its stall's jokes are considered sound.
- **Kettlesthorpe** — a hamlet of one lantern and eleven door-nails.
- **Greyling-under-Fell** — a pale town under a paling crown, saving up; the outermost ring on its gatepost is usually wet.
- **The Blackberry Mile** — a main-road leg that fruits for the well-remembered; road-memory's showpiece.
- **The Slow Stair** — the high patient road to Setterby; expects you to stop and study the joinery.
- **The Nod** — a between-road; greets you with exactly what its name says, and nothing else.
- **The Long Sight** — a crown-path reading three sheds at once; thin dawns, honest miles.

**Material grades (the courting's ladder, temperaments not tonnage):**

- **chattering** — the apprentice-stuffs; answers any hands at all, dim or bright.
- **mannered** — the road's local characters (*bell-bronze, hearth-slate*); one legible term apiece.
- **proud** — the vouching first, then terms (*singing-silver, laughing-riverwood, festival-glass*).
- **placed** — rare by where light has been, never by what ground withholds (*last-red, the twice-benched*).

(Handsel wakefulness keeps canon's own grades unchanged: **dull / warm / singing.**)

**Asking tiers (named by lantern geography, which every player can see):**

- **the low hooks** — buttons, whistles, a cracked step; a child's reach.
- **eye-height work** — kettles, wells, doors; the journeyman's day.
- **the high hooks** — a ford, a mill, a gate; work a season plans around.
- **rafters-work** — a granary roof, a river's silted voice; work the town believes of you.
- **the great askings** — never on any lantern; found on high stones, tied in red thread, sized like weather.

## PART II — THE CARD VOICE

### Who is speaking

Every card is a thing that was **given** — taught, traded, won, pressed into a palm — and so every card speaks in the one voice the fiction has already built for exactly this purpose: **the maker's own pack-notebook**, written up each night at the fire. Rules text is the maker's plain working hand. Flavor text is **the giving-line** — the sentence spoken aloud when the piece changed hands, quoted exactly, with a giving-note of where and how. The deck thereby becomes what canon says a pack is: *a life's education you can hear coming down the road* — every card in your hand has a teacher inside it, and reading your deck is reading the autobiography of everyone who ever handed you anything.

For materials and stuffs, the same notebook shifts into its field-note register — the Mannerly entry, terse as a cat-owner's wisdom: *"Likes weather-talk. Hates hurry."* One voice, two registers: the quoted warmth of the giving, the dry precision of the courting. System messages and UI copy keep the notebook's hand too — canon has already written the template (*"Wader's Ford still tells of the reel — +2"*): every mechanical event is a notebook entry with a place-name in it.

The warmth is specific and must be guarded: the notebook voice is **fond, exact, and a little amused** — a person who takes the work completely seriously and themselves not very. It never exclaims. It never addresses the player. It never lets an object talk. Whimsy lives in *what is said*, never in how hard the prose smiles.

### Sample giving-lines (seven)

1. *"Sing to the end of the pour, not the end of the song."* — the smith at Glazeby, giving me the last bars into my keeping.
2. *"The joke goes flat the moment you check whether it's working."* — folded for me by an Untold runner, who was gone by breakfast.
3. *"Dough forgives everything but hurry."* — a grandmother at the Withyfold gate, teaching with floured hands.
4. *"Tell the clay about the weather. It's been down there all year."* — first page of this notebook, in my mother's hand.
5. *"Leave the last nail for the child watching."* — a Kilnfast master, who said nothing else all day.
6. *"When the old kettle takes up its hum, you have the room."* — porch-talk at Dovelow, offered with tea.
7. *"You can't be early to a dawn. You can only be there."* — a Gleaner on the Long Sight, yawning happily at noon.

(Register check on the samples: nothing owed, due, paid, or filed; no object speaks or is named; failure-adjacent lines say *goes flat* and *goes dark*, never anything mortal; nothing is late, only out of season.)

## PART III — THE RENAMES, EXECUTED

### I-002 · 'the Fade' → **the Paling**

'The Fade' is pre-owned furniture (Dragon Age's Fade, the Wheel of Time's Padan Fain adjacency, a dozen lesser fades) and — worse — it is the one name in canon that does not obey the tongue: it is an assigned name, not a grown one. Canon has already been quietly growing the true name for two layers: *a town three seasons overdue for a maker is called pale*; *pale towns*; *the paling-arithmetic*; *a paling place*; *when the crown goes grey, the floor is saving up.* The verb is alive, the compounds already exist, and the morphology is root 1, the worked -ing, the same family as the courting and the combing.

**Candidates weighed (in the world's register):**

1. **the Paling** — grown, not assigned; verb, noun, and compounds all already canon; a child can use it (*"Emberlow's paling early this year"*). **The pick.**
2. **the Out-breath** — the light-followers' own framing (*the out-breath without which there is no in*); kept as the philosophical doublet, not the common name — nobody calls weather by its physics.
3. **the Pale-tide** — handsome, but canon already uses bare *tide* freely, and the yoke adds nothing the Paling doesn't carry lighter.
4. **the Ebbing** — right family, wrong sea; Roundelay's water imagery belongs to the round of light's groundwater, not to coasts.
5. **the Fallowing** — lovely farming register (rest so the field can grow), but it smuggles in intent — a farmer *chooses* fallow — and the Paling must stay weather, nobody's choice.

**Ruling as executed in this proposal:** the phenomenon is **the Paling** in all diegetic text; light-followers may say **the out-breath** in their own register; *the tide* remains the loose colloquial it already is. Derived forms: *paling-prone* (for Fade-prone), *the paling rate*, *palings* never pluralized (it is one weather). The style law even improves: *pale towns, the paling-arithmetic, the Paling* now form one visible word-family, so the player learns the world's biggest system by hearing one root three ways. Mechanically for the pipeline: `terms.json` gains `the-paling` with `renamed_from: the-fade`, and the canon scan rewrites `the-fade` element id to `the-paling` with the L1 fiction otherwise intact.

### I-047 · the guild-words → **wintering-town** and **the waymeet board / porch-beam**

*Guild* is a wording fossil twice over: ways keep no halls, hold no rolls, and confer nothing, so nothing in Roundelay could ever have been a guild. Executed replacements:

- **L1, "You leave the guild-town in spring"** → **"You leave your wintering-town in spring."** The wander-year already ends at one fire and begins the morning after the spring even-morrow; the town a maker winters in *is* where a run sets out from, so the coin was lying on the table. (*Home-note town* was the runner-up — beautiful, but it names the map's shape rather than the maker's life, and the tongue prefers the lived word.)
- **L2, "every schoolroom and guild-hall wall carries some folk version"** → **"every schoolroom wall and waymeet board, every porch-beam and wain-side, carries some folk version."** Canon precedent is exact: Hask keeps its riddle *on a board by the slip*; waymeet grounds keep the plank table and the fingerboard sign; porches and Fairwright wagons are where folk knowledge actually hangs. The guild-hall dissolves into the four surfaces the world really writes on.
- **Register ruling going forward:** the guild-word is retired from all diegetic text. (Non-diegetic anchor notes in L5 — *"old as guild roads," "the Wanderjahr of every guild-tale"* — are commentary about our own world's furniture and may stand, though the synthesis may scrub them for hygiene.)

### The 'turn of a Round' collision → **a verse**

L2 says *one turn of a Round* for a whole run; L5 rules *a turn is one morning wide*. The run-scale phrase gives way, per the task's instruction, and canon has already coined its replacement in the fresh-verse ruling (I-013): **a run is a verse** — *one verse of a Round*, walked by one maker; you set out on your verse, you walk your verse, and the winter-telling hands the song on mid-verse. The word is inevitable: the world is named for a round-song, a run was always a few bars carried and handed on, and *every re-spark is a fresh maker, walking a fresh verse* is already locked canon. Executed: run-scale *turn* → **verse** in all forward text; *turn* now means one worked morning only, everywhere.

## PART IV — THE STYLE LAW OF THE TWO PERSISTENCES (I-016, settled)

Canon's suggestion is kept, sharpened, and given its in-world names, because the two wakings already have a folk-taught difference (*bench-work is fired; circulation-work is held*) and the eye must learn it the same market-day way.

**The law: *if it has a line, it was fired; if it breathes, it is held; nothing carries both.***

- **Fired waking wears the drawn gold** — a single unbroken hairline of late-sun gold along the working edge and nowhere else: the lip of the bowl, the blade of the plane, the hem that does the keeping-warm. It is **the stillest thing in any frame** — a drawn line, never a glow, never a pulse, never a particle. It may be *worn* (soft and weathered on the twice-benched, a signature fading but never erased) and it blooms exactly once, at the moment of firing: a brief glaze-crackle spreading down the edge like a signature drying — beautiful, slightly final, then still forever.
- **Held waking wears the breathing gold** — a low full-surface sheen with a slow swell-and-settle rhythm (a resting breath, four to six seconds), never forming a line, brightening for one beat when a glad hand closes over it, dulling visibly in a shut box. Handsels, bright-shavings, the glad-load's seams, and the great askings all breathe; a great asking is simply the breathing gold at the scale of a hillside, which is why a found combing reads instantly as *held by a whole countryside* — the fell itself seems to rest and swell.
- **The three impossibilities, restated for artists:** people carry rim-light and never gold (the shoulder-candle, below); places carry wash and never sheen; and the unmarked wonders are the lawful limit case that proves the rule — *the drawn gold along every line at once, softly*, a fired thing whose whole surface became working edge, and even then: lines, stillness, never a breath.
- **One-glance test for any frame:** count the golds. Every still line is a fired thing; every breathing surface is a held thing; every rimmed figure is a person; every washed color is a place. If any gold both lines and breathes, the frame is wrong.

## PART V — THE VISUAL MOTIF BIBLE (named elements)

The master palette, stated once and binding: **earth and workshop colors under festival light.** Tin, straw, birch-white, dark wood, wool, slate; the bright notes are always *made* brights — madder, glaze-blue, kingfisher lining, bottle-green paint, brass — never sugar. If a color could not have come off a dyer's line or out of a kiln, it is not in Roundelay. (This is the anti-twee law in one sentence: the world is luminous because light falls on honest materials, never because the materials are candy.)

### Named elements (Layer 6)

#### `the-worked-word`

The naming language itself, kept as a living practice: the five roots (the worked -ing, the yoked pair, the temper-name, the gift-word, the homely great), and the growing of names — said plainly, repeated hearth by hearth, checked until it was so. On the Rounds, getting a thing's name right is a small act of craft like any other, and the porches are where names are proofed: say a new coin on a quiet porch and watch whether the elders pick it up or let it lie. A name the road declines simply never gets repeated, which is the only rejection Roundelay knows how to give.

- **This could become:** the writer's-room constitution for all downstream text (GDD, card names, UI copy) — a five-root checklist every new term must pass, plus a "seed packet or map poster?" smell test; procedurally, name-generation grammars for towns, pieces, and materials that compose from root-tables rather than syllable-soup, so generated content stays in-tongue.
- **Visual:** the tongue is *visible* everywhere as short words on honest surfaces — knife-cut letters in a lintel, a temper-name chalked on a jar, stitched initials in a pack-flap — never a paragraph, never a scroll; the world reads like a well-labeled workshop, not a library.

#### `the-work-names-of-things` *(the I-003 ruling)*

Nothing made is ever christened. A thing is called by its work and its place — *the Hask bell, the Setterby stair, the old kettle in the rafters, my mother's third kiln* — because nothing in Roundelay speaks except people, and a person-name is a small pretend mouth. The tongue's tenderness goes the other way: the more beloved a thing, the more exactly its work is named. Ask a maker about the plane in their pack and you will not get a pet name; you will get *the long try-plane, the one that likes quartered oak*, said the way you'd introduce a respected colleague — by what they do.

- **This could become:** a hard content rule for all card text, item text, and UI (no named NPC objects, no quoted objects, no object POV, ever), and a quiet collection-screen poetics: discovered wonders and relics list by work-and-place (*"the stair at Setterby"*), so the museum reads like a gazetteer of colleagues rather than a loot table.
- **Visual:** labels in the world are work-labels — a stall sign is a picture of the work (a bowl, a bell) with a place-word under it; woken things are painted attending, never mugging: the style guide bans faces, eyes, and mouths on any made thing, with the compass-needle tilt as the entire permitted vocabulary of object expression.

#### `the-giving-line` *(anchor — declared: the italic quotation with attribution is card-flavor furniture every deckbuilder player owns, kept deliberately familiar so its cargo reads at once: flavor text as **gift provenance** — every card quotes the person who gave it and names the giving — so the deck is an autobiography of teachers, and reading your hand is hearing everyone who ever handed you anything.)*

The card voice, in full: rules text in the maker's plain fire-written hand; flavor as the giving-line, quoted exactly, with its giving-note (*"— a grandmother at the Withyfold gate, teaching with floured hands"*); materials in the notebook's dry field-note register; system messages as notebook entries with place-names in them. One voice for the whole surface, fond, exact, a little amused — and the restraint bans enforced at the sentence level: no exclamation marks in diegetic text, no object ever quoted, nothing owed, nothing due, nothing late.

- **This could become:** the entire text layer of the GDD — card frame as notebook page; flavor slot as giving-line + giving-note; acquisition events that literally write the line at the moment of gift (the card you are taught at a gate arrives with that town's name in its note); and end-of-run, the year-string review reads the beads in the same voice, so the run's history and the deck's history are one document.
- **Visual:** the card frame is worn canvas and tool-roll leather; the painted piece sits as a *thing with a shadow*, museum-plate style; the giving-line sits below in a slanted but unlooped hand (a working italic, never cursive display); the giving-note in smaller chalk-grey. Woken cards carry the drawn gold on the painted object's working edge — on the object in the illustration, never on the card border, so the frame stays furniture and the thing stays the star.

#### `the-drawn-and-the-breathing` *(the I-016 style law, stated in Part IV, indexed here)*

Fired waking: the drawn gold — one still hairline on the working edge, blooming once like a signature drying, then the stillest thing in any frame, weathering soft on the twice-benched. Held waking: the breathing gold — full-surface sheen on a resting breath, brightening under a glad palm, dulling in a shut box, scaling from a button to a hillside (a found great asking is the breathing gold worn by a fell). *If it has a line, it was fired; if it breathes, it is held; nothing carries both.*

- **This could become:** the two persistences read at a glance across every surface — woken cards (line) vs bright handsels (breath) vs fired artifacts (line) vs glad-load bundles (breath) — with the firing moment (glaze-crackle bloom) as the game's waking juice and the breath-brighten as the payment juice; a colorblind-safe redundancy: line vs motion, not hue vs hue.
- **Visual:** gold spec: late-sun gold, varnish-over-amber, never lemon, never glitter, no particles anywhere in the system; line weight one consistent hairline at any zoom; breath rhythm 4–6 seconds, eased, never synchronized across objects in a frame (a market breathes like a crowd, not a chorus line).

#### `the-shoulder-candle`

The gleam bible — Standing worn as light, exactly as canon set it and here given its full painterly spec. The maker is lit as if by a small warm source just behind the shoulder, and the strength of that key light *is* the meter: full gleam is honeyed backlight with a long soft shadow thrown toward the viewer; middling gleam is a readable rim at dusk; near-dark is ambient light only, edges merging with the background — present, unpicked-out, never grey (the Paling has no appetite for the living, and the palette proves it: a dim maker keeps every color they own, they just stop being *lit*). Grain rides the rim as texture — long calm spans, water-ripple, freckle, watered-silk, slantwise furrow-light, bunting-dapple — the six ways' portraiture legible across a square.

- **This could become:** the Standing meter deleted from the HUD entirely — the character portrait's key light is the meter, dusk scenes as the natural "check your Standing" beat, grain-texture as the build-identity readout, and the two-paces rule staged in light: in-room gains land on the knuckles this turn; road-walking tellings land as the rim warming a shade at dusk, source-stamped in the notebook.
- **Visual:** rim only, never a gold line (channel ban held); the shadow the shoulder-candle throws is the painter's secret weapon — a bright maker's shadow reaches toward the viewer like an offered hand; light behavior on the Quiet Walk: the rim goes out, the person stays whole, and everything they ever woke keeps its gold — the game's most load-bearing lighting cue, kept exactly per canon.

#### `the-lantern-dark`

The askings bible. An asking-lantern is a chandelier of needs: dozens of small crafted objects — tin, straw, stitched cloth, bone-birch — turning slowly on red thread, backlit warm, against rafters that keep true dark. The composition is a strict three-band law: **lit hooks below** (tonight's showing), **one silhouette above them** (the next size of being believed in, shape legible, detail withheld), **honest dark above that** (the fired sizing, waiting). Each asking-object carries exactly one wrong, faded detail that states the need — the dry bowl of the spoon, the grey wing of the swallow — and that detail is always the object's *working* part: need reads as a working edge gone matte, the drawn gold's negative.

- **This could become:** the contract UI entire — tier as hook-height, escalation as silhouette-preview, the failure spiral as watching offers descend the ladder while the rafters hold; accepted askings riding the pack UI on their red thread, warming (a slow breathing-gold flush) as their season shortens — the almanac ban kept perfectly, since ripeness reads as warmth, never as a countdown.
- **Visual:** palette: folk-object naturals against blue-hour dark, one backlight source, red thread as the only saturated line; motion: slow rotation on thread, nothing else; sound motif: the lantern-mile arrives with a faint knocking of small objects in wind, wood on tin, the world's gentlest wind-chime — needs, audibly patient.

#### `rain-on-bells`

The handsel bible, and the game's sound signature. Money in graded glow — dull, warm, singing — carried visibly: net-purses, bandolier loops, coins strung at the belt, stall-fronts strung dull-to-singing like lanterns tuning. The breathing gold does the denominating; the ear does the accounting: a dull handsel clicks like a button, a warm one has a woodier note, a singing one rings — so a market at noon sounds like rain on bells, a bright maker walking is faintly audible, and a payment is a small audible chord closing in a palm. The brighten-in-the-palm is the economy's signature frame: a token flushing one shade warmer the instant a pleased hand closes over it, the world's whole theory of value in a half-second of light.

- **This could become:** currency UX with no numerals anywhere — glow-grades and chime-pitch as denominations, the wallet as a visible strung purse with a loop-count cap, idle dimming shown honestly between rests, and the glad-price read staged as canon writes it: handsels laid down one by one until the pile sings, both parties watching the coins, not each other.
- **Visual:** token shapes stay folk-made (whittled birds, glazed buttons, knots of thread) so the money never reads as coinage; glow spec per grade: dull = no sheen, matte; warm = breathing gold at low amplitude; singing = full breath plus the one permitted highlight bloom on movement; the strongbox counter-image (matte coins, faintly warmer floorboards beneath) kept as the anti-hoard poster.

#### `the-watercolor-return`

The Paling-and-dawn bible — the world's breath painted. Out-stroke: paling is desaturation with a direction, the reverse vignette — grey enters at the rooftops and edges, drains inward and downward, and the hearth-line ember holds last; knack-loss gets behavioral tells before color tells (the rope at the wrong angle, birds refusing the bridge). In-stroke: re-making re-colors as watercolor blooming across wet paper, from the worked thing outward, hearth-line first, rooftops last — the exact reverse path, so the eye learns the physics as symmetry. Return-stroke: dawns are the map's emotional weather-report — brief and ordinary over kept country; immense, long, rose-gold, almost embarrassing over pale country — and the transit law holds everywhere: nothing is ever seen moving; lanes are *found* brighter; the light arrives as weather, never as effect.

- **This could become:** node-state rendering entire — paling progress, re-color events, and dawn income all read from the same watercolor system with zero iconography; the dawn-ladder as literal sky-gradient scaling (size from paleness, length and lateness from height, per I-017); the Quill-Vale-Morning burst as the game's rarest and largest lighting event, banked for stilling releases only.
- **Visual:** paper texture lives in the scenery channel only (places are watercolor on visible grain; people and things sit crisper on top — the three settlings as three paint-handlings); grey spec: pale is *unpainted*, never dirty — the grey of paper showing through, so a pale town looks like a picture waiting to be finished, which is precisely what it is.

#### `the-four-lights-palette` *(anchor — declared: the four-season palette is furniture every player owns — green spring, gold summer, red autumn, blue winter — kept deliberately familiar so its cargo reads without a title card: the season **is** the escalation clock, palette drift is the pressure telegraph, and the year's four legs re-tune one land rather than loading four worlds.)*

#### `the-inked-hand` *(lineage, declared: this element styles Layer 5's already-declared `the-inked-round` anchor and adds the world's typography; it is not new furniture.)*

The map-and-letters bible. #### `the-courting-stance`

The materials bible — temperament as body language, read the way a cat's terms are read, nothing speaking ever. The shared motion vocabulary is small and strict: **lean** (toward the vouched-for, away from tuneless hands — minute, unmistakable), **ripple** (clay toward a singer, iron-filing tenderness), **perk** (jar contents rising a half-inch when the right maker walks in), **stiffen** (terms broken — the braid seizing the instant the song stops), **settle** (terms met — the stuff relaxing into workability like a sigh with no sound). Temperament palettes per grade: chattering stuffs in floury whites and riverbank browns that lean toward *everyone*; mannered stuffs in hearth colors with one saturated tell; proud stuffs in shy blue-whites, green-golds, and crowd-flecked clarity; placed stuffs carrying their geography visibly (last-red's lit-without-light certainty, the twice-benched's soft-worn gold).

- **This could become:** the two-gate acquisition UX with no text — the vouching read as lean (a shelf that turns toward you as Standing rises is the material market's whole menu), terms as staged performable conditions with the settle as the success frame; stall scenes as temperament dioramas the player learns to read like a farmer reads a sky.
- **Visual:** the humming stall of singing-silver as the market's quietest corner; the riverwood stall sold mid-joke; motion amplitudes capped low (a lean is degrees, not a bow) so the restraint ban holds — the moment a material's movement could read as a *gesture*, it has gone twee and must be pulled back.

#### `one-red-line`

The composition law that makes the whole bible hang together: **in any frame, at most one saturated red, and it always means minded.** Red thread on a lantern, on a found combing, at a lane's turn; the Gleaner's ember scarf; the hearth-line ember in a paling town; the madder wheels of the Fair. Red is the world's smallest vocabulary — it marks what some living regard is presently holding against the grey — so the eye learns in an hour that wherever red is, someone is minding something, and wherever it isn't, the country is either kept (colorful) or saving up (grey). One line, one meaning, no exceptions; every other warm note in the palette stays in the ambers and madders-toward-orange so the red keeps its word.

- **This could become:** the art-direction QA rule (a literal lint pass on frames: count the reds) and a quiet gameplay literacy — threat-free wayfinding where the player's eye follows red to whatever the world is holding in mind, which is always, lawfully, something askable, mindable, or about to be born on a high stone.
- **Visual:** red spec: madder and ember, never scarlet, never neon; permitted scale from a single knot (one brushstroke on unpainted paper) to red-thread-days (a grey basin filling with color from the rim inward, red threads on every high path like the first strokes of paint) — the game's finale palette is this law at festival volume.
