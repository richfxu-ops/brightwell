## THE WANDER-YEAR — what a run is, where it goes, and who hangs the need

### The run

A run is a **wander-year**: one maker's turn of one Round, walked from the morning after the spring even-morrow to the first still dawn of winter. Nobody decreed that a circuit should take a year to walk; the year trimmed the Rounds the way a river trims its banks. A circuit drawn too long starved its walkers' winters and was walked shorter; a circuit drawn too short left its towns waiting half a year between makers and was walked wider; and after enough generations every Round settled to the length of the walking season, so that the land's habit and the year's shape are now the same fact. Makers count their lives in wander-years the way trees are counted in rings, and the oldest road-courtesy question — *how many years have you walked?* — is asking about both at once.

The year is also the run's pressure, and this is the walked year's quiet mechanical gift: canon already holds that the Fade's seep runs near-still in deep winter, gentle in spring, and strongest in late summer and autumn. So a maker who sets out in the green of the year walks *into* the out-breath. The spirals turn slowly in spring and fast by the Deep Gold; the dawns thin in the Green Going and grow enormous by the Red Walk; the askings start at kettle-size partly because the world's arithmetic itself starts small. Escalating contracts are the year getting older around you — the sizing ratchet of Layer 2 and the season's rising seep, walking in step, and neither one negotiable, and neither one anyone's fault.

**A turn is one morning wide.** Dawn issues the day's regard; the maker stands in it, walks, works, courts, answers; and the turn closes at a fire — a hearth claimed, a porch joined, a camp made — where the day's telling is given or kept. One dawn, one road, one fire: every mechanical turn of the game is a day with this exact silhouette, and the world never needs another clock.

**A leg is the road between two high days.** The year keeps a small string of fixed lights everyone walks toward — the two even-morrows at the equinoxes, the Long Day of midsummer, the first-frost fires, the Round-Fair's passage through each country — and the stretch between one high day and the next is a leg: a handful of turns, a season's errand, a chapter with weather for a title. Makers plan in legs, tell in legs, and are asked at hearths not "where have you been" but "*what did you walk between the lights?*"

**How it starts:** see `the-setting-out` below. No guild assigns, no elder appoints, no paper exists to be signed anywhere in the world. A fresh maker is sparked the only way gleam starts anywhere: by a first delight given, usually on waymeet ground on the morning after the spring even-morrow, when the tide of the year turns to the in-breath and the lanterns hang their lowest hooks with the smallest askings of the season.

**How it ends, twice:** every wander-year has two possible endings, and both are kind, and only one is bright. If the light goes — Standing exhausted, per the locked spine — the year ends where it stands, in any season, and the maker walks the Quiet Walk exactly as canon holds it: no pack, no pieces, no askings, fed and warmed and asked for nothing, the circuit walked in reverse light. On the road they call this ending *walking out of season*, because the Quiet Walk keeps no calendar; it is the one journey in Roundelay the year does not shape. But if the maker is still lit when the first still dawn comes — when the seep goes near-still and the roads go white and the year itself stops asking — the wander-year ends in triumph, and triumph in Roundelay has a shape as specific as the Quiet Walk's: **the winter-telling** (below), one hearth, one fire, the whole year told, and the Round handed on mid-song to whoever sets out in spring.

### The map

A Round is a loop, never a tree: a circuit with a home note, exactly as Layer 1 locked it. What Layer 5 adds is the texture of the beads that loop strings — and the law that the map is a calendar laid on land, so that reading the one is reading the other.

The node types a route threads (full grammar at `the-threaded-round`): **bright towns**, kept and colored, small askings and ordinary dawns; **pale towns**, spiral-chalked and patient, big askings and rose-gold mornings; **hearth-steads and porches**, the fires that close a turn; **waymeets**, where circuits cross and the ways mix and the tables stand; **fell crossings and high shrines**, the fast-paling heights where the dawn-ladder tops out; **vale floors**, apprentice-stuff country, where the gloaming-table runs shallow and re-makings take root quickest; **the between-roads**, the Untold's rumor-silent hedge-lines, no askings and no tales carried; **gleaning country**, the long grey left-behind roads, rich in last-red exactly as far as the quiet goes; **the Round-Fair**, the one node that moves, riding a week ahead of autumn's front; **the wonders**, closed rounds standing in the land like colleagues, route-magnets for every way at once; and, off every main thread, countable on one hand across all the Rounds, **the still bowls**.

Choosing a route feels like neither shopping nor triage. It feels like *threading* — beads chosen and strung in the order the year will meet them — and like reading an almanac forward: because the spirals compound at a chalked rate, because tellings walk at a posted pace, because autumn's front crosses the country the same way every year, a maker standing at a waymeet in spring can read most of their autumn in the land around them. The weight of the choice is the Layer 3 mending question worn as a season: answer the three-ring town now, small and sure, or thread wide of it and let it ripen toward the poem — knowing the year's arithmetic will hold you to whichever kind of maker you turn out to be.

And the pressure is readable on the land itself, always, without one interface lie: gatepost spirals ring by ring; road-marks copying a town's count two valleys forward so the road knows before the hill's crest; red thread knotted where one knack is minded against a return; dawn-color as the honest weather-report of the table below (the rosier the morning, the paler the country); the reverse-vignette grey of a paling town, rooftops first, hearth-line last; the Fair's dust on the seam of the seasons; and at dusk, the maker's own pack tilting toward whatever road their light is walking in on.

### Who hangs the need

Three issuers, one traveling variant, and no fourth — Roundelay keeps no office that could hold one.

1. **Towns, through their asking-lanterns.** The ordinary tier, exactly as canon lays it down: the crafted need-object on red thread, the lantern showing the town's settled belief, sizing fired to the brightest the road has heard you be, showing gated by tonight's gleam. *Witnessed by* the square's crowd and the town's woken things — the only court there is; *received by* the town, which keeps what woke; and answered work echoes outward as the glad-load through the gate and road-marks chalked a day's walk ahead.
2. **The countryside, through the combing.** The great tier, and canon is strict that no one issues it and no one may: a country's crossed and living regard combs the loose wash into a found arrangement, red thread is tied, and red-thread-days gather the six ways around the chosen hands. *Witnessed by* the gathered bench — the biggest audience of a walking life; *received by* the season itself, whose missing is handed back shaped.
3. **Materials, through the courting's terms.** The smallest tier, already law in Layer 4: a term accepted is an asking like any other and can go stale like any other. Every jar in the pack is a contract the size of a song. *Witnessed by* the stuff itself and whatever attends the bench; *received by* the work.
4. **The Round-Fair, through the traveling lantern** — the one variant this layer adds, small and lawful: the Fair keeps a lantern on its tallest wagon, the only lantern in the world that moves, and it hangs the Fair's own needs — a ground to rake, a stage-turn to keep, a ring to raise before the wagons roll. A traveling lantern's readings travel with it, which makes it the one lantern a maker meets twice in a year, and the only one whose rafters follow you (see `a-week-ahead-of-autumn`).

Finished work is received the way everything in Roundelay is received — by delight, never by sign-off: the town keeps what woke, the glad-load loads the pack, the tellings set out walking, and the answered asking-object comes down off the lantern *with its thread*, to join the maker's year-string (below), which is how a run wears its own history where everyone can read it.

## Carried issues, answered in-fiction

**I-017 — the elevation law, in one line:** ***Dawn climbs back up the channels the seep cut down, and every ground is repaid in the measure it sent.*** The seep walks downhill by dusk — so the high and thin-lived pale fastest (a fell shrine has a whole slope pulling at its wash and no nightly hearth re-binding it), and the vale floors hold their color longest, sitting over the table like orchards over groundwater. But the reissue is not a flat flood: dawn rises out of the table and spends itself back along the very grain of the seep, each ground taking its share *as the rising passes, in the measure it sent down*. So the vale floor gets its share at first light, brief where the country is kept; and the pale heights — which sent the most, farthest — are repaid biggest and longest, the return climbing the slope through the whole morning until the grey crowns stand in rose-gold after the valley has already gone to work. Which is why both folk-saws were always the same finding: *valleys hold their reds* and *pale country gets the best mornings* are the out-stroke and the return-stroke of one seep, and even the sun agrees — first light has always loved a hilltop. Paleness sets the size of a node's dawn; height sets its length and lateness; the seep sets both.

**I-030 — the speed of regard, in one line:** ***Light crosses a room at once, and a country only on foot.*** Present overflow lands instantly where it is caused, because a cup that runs over runs onto the table it stands on: the Moth Fair's four hundred were *in the room*, and the light went up the strings that hour. Distant delight has no line to run along until a telling lays one, fireside by fireside, one hearth a night — the walking light of Layer 4, unedited. Geography reads the law plainly: the map has exactly two speeds, the room's and the road's, and no third courier has ever existed or ever will. What the crowd in front of you feels, you wear tonight; what a far town comes to feel, you will wear when its telling arrives and is seen landing, and not one dusk sooner.

**I-043 — the stilled summer, in geography:** a stilling only ever takes a **one-throat bowl** — a closed round vale whose entire seep must pass a single narrow throat — and the light-followers note, carefully, that in each of living memory's three stillings a near-closed wonder stood in or above that throat, and say no more than that. The rarity is therefore a rarity of coincidence in landform: one-throat bowls are scarce as perfect things are scarce, countable on one hand across all the Rounds; the near-closures are a short beloved list; and the two standing together is rarer than a new closed round being found. No great circuit runs *through* such a bowl — the Rounds settled along the seep's easy grain generations ago, and a one-throat vale is precisely where the grain pinches — so a stilling is never met on the thread of a route, only ever by a chosen detour, and most makers walk every wander-year of a long life and never once stand in varnish-light. Three stillings in living memory; two of them seen by nobody now walking.

## Named elements (Layer 5)

### `the-wander-year` *(anchor — declared: the journeyman's traveling year is folk furniture old as guild roads — the young crafter who must walk strange towns for a year and a day before mastery — kept deliberately familiar so that its cargo reads at once: the run-as-year identity, escalation as the season's own rising weather, and the calendar as the run's master resource.)*

One maker's turn of one Round: out with the in-breath on the morning after the spring even-morrow, home to a fire — one way or the other — by the first still dawn of winter. The year trimmed the circuits to its own length long ago, so the run and the calendar are one shape and one clock: the seep near-still when the pack is light, strongest when the pack is bright and the rafters heavy, the whole locked spine of escalation worn as nothing more sinister than the world getting later. Makers count their lives in wander-years; roads count makers the same way; and the porches say the year's whole strategy in one sentence: *spring is for learning the road, summer is for being learned by it, and autumn is for being worth what it heard.*

- **This could become:** the run-frame itself — run length fixed by the calendar track (spring→first frost), turn = one day, leg = road between high days; the seasonal Fade-rate curve doubling lawfully as the D-001 escalation curve (pressure rises because the season does); two run-end triggers, calendar (triumph, graded by the year's crown) and Standing-zero (Quiet Walk, any season).
- **Visual:** the circuit map drawn as a year-wheel — one ring of painted land quartered by the four lights, the maker's inked line growing around it like a tree-ring, dawn-gilding on whichever arc of the wheel the season currently touches.

### `the-setting-out`

Runs begin the way gleam begins: with a first delight given. On the morning after the spring even-morrow, waymeet grounds all along the Rounds hold the oldest small ceremony on the road — no rite, no roll, nothing conferred, in keeping with everything Layer 3 locked — just the **first-gift**: a fresh maker makes some small true thing in front of whoever happens to be on the ground, porch elders and yawning festival-keepers and other setters-out, and whatever thread of shine runs back from their delight is the whole of a starting stake. Then the nearest town's lantern takes its first reading of those hands — fired, small, a kettle at eye height — the road chalks a first mark for them, and the year has begun. Masters' season-shadows step off half a stride behind, per canon. And there are out-of-season sparks, honored and slightly legendary: a Quiet Walker re-lit by laughter in a stranger's kitchen sets out from that kitchen whenever the flicker comes, walking what the porches call *a spring of one*.

- **This could become:** the run-start as diegetic loadout — what you choose to make as first-gift (and who happens to be watching) seeds starting deck grain, way-affinity, and the first lantern reading; out-of-season starts as unlockable alternate openings with off-calendar tunings.
- **Visual:** waymeet ground at first light, dew on the plank table, a loose half-circle of strangers with tea, one small made thing changing hands — and the year's first thread of shine landing on a stranger's knuckles like the first ray over the hill.

### `the-four-lights`

The walked year has four legs, named the way everything on the Rounds is named — by light and weather — and no maker has ever needed them explained. **The Green Going** (spring): seep gentle, spirals slow, askings kettle-sized, dawns thin, hedges kind; the leg for learning what this year's valleys answer to. **The Long Light** (high summer): the year's biggest working days, benches at dusk still warm, the mending question asked at every gate — answer now, or let it ripen. **The Deep Gold** (late summer): the out-breath leaning in; spirals turning visibly now, gleaning country opening, honey-light and long shadows and the first enormous dawns. **The Red Walk** (autumn): the seep at full, the Fair a week ahead of the front, the countryside's missing gathering toward wherever the year's arithmetic says seven — the leg every other leg was quietly aiming at. Then **the Wintering**, unwalked: the world's near-still month(s), when roads whiten, lanterns hold their dark, and whatever a maker still carries is carried to one fire. The high days stitch the legs together — even-morrow to Long Day to first-frost fires — and because a crowd is an occasion, not a faucet, the high days are also the year's great gathered audiences: the calendar is a resource because the watchers live on it.

- **This could become:** four-act run structure on one map — same land, lawfully re-tuned per leg (Fade rate, dawn richness, crowd availability, stall-warmth), with high days as fixed calendar nodes granting the run's largest venues; act transitions telegraphed entirely by palette and weather, never by title card.
- **Visual:** one hill painted four times: new-leaf green under rain-grey; white-gold and dust with short noon shadows; honey and amber raking sideways; madder-red thread on grey under a rose-gold dawn — the game's seasonal palette bible in a single four-panel plate.

### `the-threaded-round`

A route is beads on a thread, and every maker plans one the same way: thread in hand — often literally, the red spool at the belt — over a year-wheel map, choosing the order the year will meet its beads. The bead-box holds: bright towns and pale (the same town, different years); hearth-steads and porches to close the turns; waymeets where the ways mix and the tables stand; fell crossings and high shrines, cold, fast-paling, crowned in long dawns; vale floors where clay chatters and re-makings root; between-roads, quiet as hedges, carrying no askings and no tales; gleaning country down the long grey left-behind roads; the moving Fair; the standing wonders; and — never on the thread, only ever a chosen swerve off it — a still bowl. Threading is the game's whole strategy felt as craft: you cannot walk every bead, the year meets each one exactly once, and the ones you thread wide of go on ripening at the chalked rate, so that choosing a route is quietly authoring your own autumn. Nobody has ever called it triage, because it isn't: it is deciding which of a country's needs will get the *best* of you, and trusting the rest to the next verse — the mending question, worn as a map.

- **This could become:** the map-generation grammar — a loop of typed beads stamped with elevation, season-of-arrival, and spiral state; route commitment in arcs (choose the next leg's beads at each waymeet, never the whole year at once); skipped beads compounding visibly per the paling-arithmetic.
- **Visual:** the route-planning screen as an actual strung thread: carved beads in distinct silhouettes — lantern, porch, plank table, fell-cairn, green wagon, bell — held up against the painted land they stand for, the red thread slack where the choice is still open.

### `the-lantern-mile`

The last mile into any town reads the whole town, and every maker walks it the same way: eyes up. Road-marks thicken toward a gate like print toward a title — a spiral copied forward on a stile two valleys back, now confirmed ring for ring on the gatepost itself; red thread knotted at the lane's turn where somebody is minding the well's one knack against a return; the dawn that morning already told you the table's fullness in rose; the rooftops say the rest in the reverse-vignette grey of a paling place, color draining crown-first, hearth-line last. And then the lantern, visible from the ridge: how low the low hooks hang, how heavy the rafters sit, dozens of small crafted needs turning on red thread, backlit — a town's whole state legible from a hundred yards by anyone the road has taught. Makers say a good lantern-mile *tells you the size of your welcome before it tells you the size of the work*, and both readings are always true, because both are the same light.

- **This could become:** the node-preview layer, fully diegetic — the approach screen IS the information screen: spiral count, asking-tier silhouettes in the rafters, dawn-richness, stall-warmth, all read off the landscape art before entry, no menus anywhere; withheld information only where the world lawfully withholds it (rafters in shadow above tonight's gleam).
- **Visual:** ridge-top dusk view down a hedged mile: chalk birds on stiles, the spiral gatepost with its outermost ring still wet, one red knot at the lane's elbow, and at the end the asking-lantern glowing like a dropped constellation — the game's most repeated and most load-bearing establishing shot.

### `the-dawn-ladder`

The elevation law, stated the way a light-follower would chalk it: **dawn climbs back up the channels the seep cut down, and every ground is repaid in the measure it sent.** By dusk the seep walks downhill — so the heights pale first, having a whole slope's pull on their wash and no nightly hearth to re-bind it, and the vale floors hold their reds, sitting over the gloaming-table like orchards over groundwater. By dawn the same channels run in reverse: the reissue rises through the vale at first light and climbs the slope's grain all morning, each ground taking its share as the rising passes — the kept country's share brief and ordinary, the pale country's share vast, and the pale *heights*' share vast and long and late, first-lit and last-left, because they sent the most the farthest down. Gleaners camp on grey fells above full vales for exactly this: the long rose-gold morning that starts at the valley mist and ends at their boots. Even the sun keeps the law's appearance — first light has always loved a hilltop — and the light-followers enjoy that agreement without needing it: their chalk says the return follows the seep's own grain, and the seep says everything else.

- **This could become:** elevation as a real stat on every node — Fade-rate modifier (higher = faster paling) and dawn-income shape (size from paleness, length and lateness from height above the local table); dawn-chasing as a route archetype (high camps over pale catchments, priced honestly by the fact that the catchment paled because you let it); vale routes as the steady counter-play (cheap re-makings, small sure mornings).
- **Visual:** a naturalist's cutaway plate of one slope at two hours — dusk: gold veins seeping down under a greying fell into the pooled table beneath the orchard vale; dawn: the same veins running in reverse, rose-gold climbing the hillside's grain until the pale crown burns longest — the world's kindest diagram, chalked in every schoolroom.

### `the-room-and-the-road`

The speed of light, as the Rounds actually live it: **light crosses a room at once, and a country only on foot.** When delight overfills before your eyes, the overflow lands that hour — a cup runs over onto the table it stands on, the Moth Fair ran up the strings before the reel ended — so everything the crowd in front of you feels, you wear by dusk, and the run's great Standing swings always happen where the maker is standing, in public, at the top of the engine. Everything else must be *carried*: a far town's delight has no line to run along until a telling lays one, fireside by fireside, one hearth a night, exactly at the walking light's posted pace. The map therefore keeps two speeds and no third, ever: the room's flare and the road's fuse. A maker reads both at a glance — tonight's crowd in the square, and the string of hearths behind them lighting one per night toward their pack — and plans a year around the difference, because the Untold outwalk their own tellings on rumor-silent roads while a Fairwright's good ground brightens him all winter, and both are the same law at two speeds.

- **This could become:** the Standing interface split lawfully in two — instant resolution at the current node (the crowd's response lands this turn), plus inbound walking light drawn as tellings moving hearth-to-hearth along map roads with readable arrival dusks (per Layer 4's `the-walking-light`, unedited); no gain and no sizing ever moves off-screen.
- **Visual:** the night map: one bright flare where the maker stands, and far back along the road a string of small hearth-fires lighting one per night toward them — a slow fuse of warmth crossing a dark country, the game's tenderest ambient animation.

### `a-week-ahead-of-autumn`

Autumn crosses Roundelay as a front, the way autumn crosses any country: it takes the heights first — the same slopes the seep drains fastest — and walks down into the vales over weeks, a moving seam of deepening gold and strengthening seep that anyone can see from a hilltop. The Round-Fair rides exactly one week ahead of that seam, always has, wagons keeping the season's pace the way gulls keep a plow's, so that the Fair's track for the whole year is readable in spring by anyone who knows which way autumn walks — and every maker's threading includes, somewhere, the intercept: where on the wheel will I meet the wagons? And on the tallest wagon hangs the Fair's own asking-lantern, the only lantern in the world that travels. It hangs the Fair's needs — a ground to rake before the crowds come, a stage-turn to keep, a ring to raise — and because a lantern's readings ride with it, it is the one lantern that meets a maker twice in one year and remembers: the rafters that watched you in the Green Going are the rafters that greet you in the Red Walk, sized to everything the road has heard between.

- **This could become:** the moving hub node with a fully predictable track (the autumn front as a visible, advancing map gradient); fair-intercept as a core routing subgame (chase it, cut across its arc, or let it come to you at compounded cost elsewhere); the traveling lantern as the run's one persistent contract source, its sizings following the player all year — the game's most personal ratchet.
- **Visual:** from a hilltop: green-gold country ahead of the seam, turning country behind it, and on the road exactly between, a line of bottle-green wagons trailing dust and bunting along the border of two seasons — arriving, as canon insists, audibly, like rain on bells getting closer.

### `the-still-bowls`

Where stillings happen, when they happen at all: a **one-throat bowl**, the rarest landform on the Rounds — a closed round vale whose whole seep must pass one narrow throat — with, in every stilling living memory holds, a near-closed wonder standing in or above that throat; the light-followers note the coincidence carefully, chalk it, and say no more than that, because the how of it sits on the far side of the same horizon as every closed round's beginning. One-throat bowls are countable on one hand across all the Rounds; the near-closures are a short beloved list; the two together are rarer than a new closed round being found. And the great circuits, which settled along the seep's easy grain generations before anyone now walking, all pass such bowls *by*: a stilling is never on the thread, only ever a chosen swerve, a detour into varnish-light where nothing pales and nothing returns and the birds sing confused at noon. Three stillings in living memory. Most makers walk every wander-year of a long life and never cross one; the ones who have all tell you the same two things — that the grey mornings inside were the loneliest weather of their lives, and that they would walk a season out of their way to stand in the turning dawn again.

- **This could become:** the rare anomaly region of Layer 4's `the-stilled-summer`, given its spawn law — only in one-throat-bowl terrain adjacent to a wonder landmark, roughly once per several runs, always off the main loop so crossing is a chosen detour; the equinox release date visible on the calendar track, making the whole bowl one routing gamble about *being there when it turns*.
- **Visual:** a round vale like a held breath — one narrow rocky throat with an old wonder in it, softly edge-lit along every line — waxy fruit-colors and light like varnish inside; and at the equinox, the whole bowl's withheld rose-gold pouring out through the throat at dawn like a tipped cup, three towns' worth of small figures standing still in it.

### `the-crown-of-the-year`

Every wander-year has a crown, and the maker spends the year deciding — without once being asked — where it will sit. The Red Walk is combing weather: the seep at its strongest is when seventh rings close, so the countrysides a maker threaded wide of all year do their arithmetic in autumn, and wherever the year's own skips converge, the wash combs, the red thread is tied, and red-thread-days gather the six ways around the chosen hands exactly as Layer 3 holds. The crown is therefore never a fixture and never a surprise: it is the place-and-time the whole year walked toward, authored leg by leg by the maker's own threading, telegraphed for seasons by converging spirals and Gleaner hazel, and arriving — like everything in this world — as weather the player routed. And in the small years, when no country crests, the crown is quieter and still a crown: a five-ring town answered as a poem before two hundred dawn-watchers, or the Fair's last ground of the season, the stage-turn the whole wheel's tellings come home to. The porches grade nothing and remember everything, and their word for a maker's best autumn work is simply *her crown, the year she walked the west wheel*.

- **This could become:** the finale-scheduling law — boss location computed from the player's own skip-arithmetic (the region whose summed spirals crest in autumn), telegraphed for seasons on the map; a guaranteed year-end finale whose tier scales honestly to the year's actual weather: great asking with gathered bench, five-ring poem, or fair's-last-stage — no run ends without its crown, and no crown was ever imposed.
- **Visual:** the year-wheel map in autumn with every chalked spiral drawn live, one countryside where the rings visibly converge like iron filings finding a magnet — and then canon's red-thread-days plate: a grey valley filling with color from the edges inward, wagons on every road, one small bright-rimmed figure at the middle, pack open, looking up.

### `the-year-string`

When an answered asking comes down off a lantern, it comes down *with its thread*, and every maker does with it what makers have always done: strings it. Across the pack-flap, bead by bead, the wander-year accumulates in the open — the tin spoon from the Green Going, the whistle from the fair, the stitched swallow that took the whole Deep Gold — a run's history worn where any hearth can read it, in the one shorthand the whole road shares. The string aims eyes and moves nothing: lanterns read grain, not souvenirs, so the year-string raises no sizing and carries no telling — it is for *people*, the way all beads are, a conversation hung on a pack, the first thing children ask about and the last thing a winter fire lets you skip. And when a light goes out on the road, the custom is the road's gentlest: the Quiet Walker hangs their string on the lantern where the year ended, and walks on unweighted, and every maker who passes that gate reads the beads and warms the story back to life — which is, the porches point out, only the waymeet table's law worn one size sadder: what you will not be keeping bright, you leave where it will be.

- **This could become:** the run-history UI as a physical object — contracts completed strung visibly on the pack, each bead a tooltip of its own telling; end-of-run review read bead by bead; one bead chosen at run's end (winter-telling or Quiet Walk epilogue alike) to persist into the meta-layer, unifying the hearth-right telling, the Quiet Walk's chosen work, and run scoring into a single diegetic gesture.
- **Visual:** close-up of a worn pack-flap: red thread strung with a tin spoon, a folded paper bird, a whistle, a stitched swallow with both wings bright now — each bead carrying its small gold working-edge; and the counter-image, the road's quiet memorial: a string swaying on a lantern-post at dusk, beads turning slowly, story intact.

### `the-winter-telling` *(anchor — declared: the tale told by the winter fire is hearth-furniture every player owns — the traveler home from the road, the long night, the listening house — kept deliberately familiar so that its cargo reads in one amber frame: the triumphant run-end as a **telling**, meta-progression as story-persistence, and the handing-on of the Round mid-song.)*

The bright ending. A maker still lit at the first still dawn walks to one fire — a porch they love, a town that loves them, a waymeet's winter camp — and winters there as the honored teller, and the whole wander-year is told across the still months, leg by leg, bead by bead off the year-string, to a house that wakes richer for every night of it. This is what triumph *is* in Roundelay: not a treasury and not a title, but a year good enough to keep a house warm all winter in the telling, worn on the skin as the shine the tellings hold there. And the winter-telling ends the only way a Round can end — by not ending: at the spring even-morrow the teller rises as the road's newest master, some green shadow already falling in half a stride behind, or sits down on the porch for good and becomes the audience every future maker plays to; and one bead of the year, the best-loved telling of the winter, goes on walking without them, hearth by hearth into the next verse, which is the oldest saying on the Rounds kept literally: *you never finish a Round; you hand it on mid-song.*

- **This could become:** the victory sequence as a playable epilogue — choose the wintering fire (porch, town, or waymeet, each seeding a different meta-reward: a new porch elder, a town relationship, a card left in the traveling pool), then choose the bead that goes on walking (the run's one persistent gift); new-game-plus framed as the next verse, with the shelved same-maker continuation mode (Layer 2's fired-readings option) available as the explicitly named high-stakes alternative.
- **Visual:** deep winter, blue snow-light at the window, amber everywhere else; the maker mid-tale with the year-string held up to the firelight, every woken thing in the rafters leaned a degree closer — and through the window, far off on the white hill, the thin green thread of next spring's road.

## Register check

Nothing in this layer is owed, filed, scheduled, or adjudicated: routes are threaded, never booked; askings warm and stale by season and are never "due"; the crown is authored by weather and threading, never assigned; the winter-telling is warmth, never a wage. Danger on the road is dimming, staleness, weather, and the land's strangeness — the greyest nodes in the game are the loneliest, never the deadliest, and no bead in the box is a den, a ruin, or an ambush. The grammar of light holds: paling is scenery's, gleam is the maker's, gold edges stay on the beads. The courier law holds: the year-string and all road-marks aim eyes and move no reading; tellings alone move light, at one hearth a night; the room's instant overflow adds no third courier, being canon's own Moth Fair condition restated as geography.

### Register bans proposed forward (Layer 5 additions, binding on Layers 6–7)

- **Almanac ban:** the year keeps high days and seasons, never dates-owed. No diegetic deadline, appointment, or itinerary vocabulary; an asking warms, ripens, and stales — it is never *due*, and nothing in the world is ever *late*, only *out of season*.
- **Weather-danger law:** no node type may host combat-default, ambush, ruin-delving, or lurking-monster framing. The road's hazards are the seep, the staling, the weather, and the strangeness of still bowls — and the loneliest country in the game is grey, not hostile.
- **Crown guard:** the great asking is never annual, sited in advance, or institutional. The crown emerges from the player's own skip-arithmetic and the season's weather, every year, or arrives quieter — no layer may fix it to a festival or a place.
